Understanding “Can” and “Can’t”

In English, the modal verb can is used to talk about abilities, possibilities, and permissions. Its negative form is can’t (contraction of cannot) and it is used to express inability, impossibility, or prohibition.

Structure

The grammatical structure with can and can’t is very simple: 

 Subject + can/can’t + base verb + complement

Examples:

  • I can swim. → I am able to swim.
  • She can’t drive. → She is not able to drive.

Uses of “Can”

  • Ability: I can play the piano. (I know how to play the piano)
  • Possibility: We can go to the park tomorrow. (It is possible for us to go to the park tomorrow)
  • Permission: You can use my phone. (You are allowed to use my phone)

Uses of “Can’t”

  • Inability: I can’t speak Japanese. (I am not able to speak Japanese)
  • Impossibility: Pigs can’t fly. (It’s impossible for pigs to fly)
  • Prohibition: You can’t park here. (It’s not allowed to park here)

Questions with “Can”

To make questions, the order of the subject and the modal verb is inverted: 

 Can + subject + base verb + complement?
  • Can you dance? → Are you able to dance?
  • Can he drive a car? → Is he able to drive a car?

The most common short answers are:

  • Yes, I can.
  • No, I can’t.

In summary, can and can’t are used with the base form of the verb (without “to”) and they do not change with the subject. Example: I can run | He can run | They can run
